### How Evidence Sources Converged or Conflicted

**Strong Convergence:**

All four evidence types consistently support the core Compensation ‚Üí Motivation ‚Üí Job Satisfaction hypothesis:

- **Scientific Evidence (Ghana & Indonesia studies):** Validated the theoretical mechanism across different cultures (West Africa, Southeast Asia) and industries (banking, retail), confirming that compensation impacts motivation and motivation mediates the satisfaction relationship

- **Practitioner Evidence (Technology, Financial Services, Professional Services):** Real-world implementations showed 15-25% satisfaction improvements when compensation systems were redesigned with performance-linkage and transparency, confirming the pathway works in practice

- **Organizational Evidence (N=2,847 employees):** Our internal data showed the strongest correlations - r=0.58 (compensation-satisfaction), r=0.42 (compensation-motivation), r=0.71 (motivation-satisfaction) - all significant at p<0.001, validating the complete mediation model with 31% variance explained

- **Stakeholder Evidence (~45 perspectives):** All stakeholder groups (leadership, finance, employees, management, HR) recognized the compensation-satisfaction problem exists and confirmed employees report pay-performance disconnect lowers motivation and satisfaction

**Key Convergence Points:**
1. **Motivation as Mediator:** Scientific research (PT. Pelita mediation study) and organizational data (31% variance explained) both confirm motivation is the mechanism linking compensation to satisfaction
2. **Temporal Validation:** Organizational data showing 18% satisfaction decline during salary freeze and 8% recovery after adjustments aligns with scientific and practitioner findings about compensation's direct impact
3. **Success Factors:** Practitioners and stakeholders both emphasized transparency and performance-linkage as critical, confirming the mechanism requires more than just pay increases

**Areas of Divergence/Tension:**

While all evidence supports the hypothesis, some tensions emerged:

1. **Mechanism Strength vs. Implementation Feasibility:** Organizational evidence shows very strong correlations (r=0.58, r=0.71) suggesting compensation interventions should work well, BUT stakeholder evidence reveals implementation barriers (finance gatekeeping, ROI requirements, budget constraints) that may limit practical application despite strong underlying relationships

3. **Explained vs. Unexplained Variance:** Organizational data shows compensation explains only 34% of satisfaction variance (66% unexplained), suggesting other factors matter significantly - this moderates confidence despite strong correlations, indicating compensation alone won't solve all satisfaction issues

**Resolution of Tensions:**

The evidence integration reveals that the compensation ‚Üí motivation ‚Üí job satisfaction relationship is REAL and STRONG (supported by all four sources), but:
- Implementation requires attention to transparency, performance-linkage, and stakeholder buy-in (practitioner/stakeholder insights)
- Compensation is an important but not singular driver of satisfaction (organizational 66% unexplained variance)
- Success depends on execution quality, not just implementing compensation changes (practitioner lessons)
